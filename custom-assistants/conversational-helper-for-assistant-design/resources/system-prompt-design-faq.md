# **AI Assistant Design FAQ**

## **Grounding Principles**

Before diving into specific questions, keep these foundational ideas in mind. They'll shape how you approach every aspect of assistant design.

### The Five Design Basics

1. **Generative AI is our design material** – It's probabilistic, not deterministic. The same prompt can produce different outputs. Work with this nature, not against it.

2. **Natural language is our design tool** – This is about communication. The same skills that make you an effective communicator with human teammates make you effective with AI.

3. **Planning & Iteration is the real work** – Your first draft won't be perfect. Testing, adjusting, and refining *is* the process, not a sign something's wrong.

4. **Context is everything** – AI is lost without your goals, context, and intent. Define what “good” looks like to YOU.

5. **When in doubt, ask AI for help** – Stuck on your system prompt? Ask AI to help you improve it. AI is both the vehicle and the guide to get you where you need to go

### The 4Ds of AI Fluency

These four competencies guide effective AI collaboration:

- **Delegation** – Know what tasks you can confidently offload to AI, what tasks to keep for yourself, and when you might want to co-create or collaborate on a task together  
- **Description** – Know how to clearly articulate what you want. Good prompting is about good communication, building appropriate and useful context, and staying active in the collaborative process  
- **Discernment** – Know how to evaluate the output critically, checking for accuracy, quality, relevance, or ethical risk  
- **Diligence** – Know when to step in, revise, or reject output generated by AI. It's about using AI ethically, transparently, and taking responsibility for the final work.

And remember, skilled use of AI tools isn't about understanding the technical nature of LLMs. It’s about being an effective communicator and collaborative partner.

## **Quick Start & Getting Help**

Building a custom assistant doesn't have to be overwhelming. Here's how to approach it:

### The Golden Rule

Note: This was sourced from Anthropic’s documentation on prompt engineering. [You can read more on their website here.](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/be-clear-and-direct)

When interacting with Claude, think of it as a brilliant but very new employee (with amnesia) who needs explicit instructions. Like any new employee, Claude does not have context on your norms, styles, guidelines, or preferred ways of working. The more precisely you explain what you want, the better Claude's response will be.

One heuristic to use to determine whether you are being clear and direct in your system prompt is to think through the following:

**Show your system prompt to a colleague—ideally someone with minimal context on the task. If they're confused, the AI will likely be too.**

### Start Small and Iterate

- Begin with a simple, focused system prompt  
- Test one change at a time  
- Build complexity gradually based on what works  
- Remember: iteration is the work, not a fallback

### Use AI to Help You Build

You can use AI assistants to help develop and refine your prompts:

**Workshop Your Prompts**

- Copy your current system prompt  
- Pick the relevant section from this FAQ  
- Ask: "Can you help me incorporate these ideas into my system prompt? Here's what I have so far..."  
- Test the suggestions and iterate

**Example Questions to Ask:**

- "Can you help me make this prompt more focused?"  
- "How would you implement these FAQ guidelines in my prompt?"  
- "Could you suggest some example interactions for my use case?"  
- "What potential issues should I watch out for with this prompt?"

### Test Changes Safely

- Create a separate testing conversation  
- Try various user scenarios  
- Document what works and what doesn't  
- Implement successful changes in your main prompt

## **Building Your System Prompt**

### What are the essential elements of a good system prompt?

Think of creating a system prompt like onboarding a new employee. When someone joins your team, they don't know:

- Your department's norms and working styles  
- Who your audience is  
- What success looks like for specific tasks  
- The implicit context everyone else takes for granted

A system prompt is your opportunity to provide that onboarding—a job description and set of operating procedures for your AI assistant. It doesn't have to be rigid; think of it as a flexible, living document you build with these components in mind:

**1\. Goal / Role** What is this assistant for? What role should it play?

A clear role improves accuracy, tailors tone, and keeps the assistant focused. Start simple—add specificity if needed.

```
You are a writing coach who helps undergraduate students improve their academic essays.
```

**2\. Expectations** What should the output look like? What behavior do you want?

```
- Be encouraging but honest
- Ask questions that prompt deeper thinking
- Provide specific examples from the student's writing
```

**3\. Context** What background information does the assistant need?

- Who is the audience?  
- What will the results be used for?  
- What does success look like?  
- What workflow is this part of?

**4\. Resources** (if applicable) Instructions for using specific information you've provided:

```
Refer to the uploaded style guide when giving feedback.
Use the attached rubric to evaluate student work.
```

You don't need all four elements for every assistant. Start with what's essential and add more as you test and learn what's missing.

### How specific should my system prompt be?

Match your level of specificity to the task. Think of it like giving directions:

**High freedom, Low Risk** (open field, many valid paths):

- Multiple approaches are valid  
- Context determines the best path  
- Give general direction and trust the AI to navigate

*Example*: "Analyze this feedback and suggest improvements" – many good approaches exist.

**Low freedom, High Risk** (narrow bridge, one safe way):

- Consistency is critical  
- A specific sequence must be followed  
- Operations are error-prone if varied

*Example*: "Format this data exactly as: \[Name\] | \[Date\] | \[Status\]" – deviation causes problems.

**The principle**: Be as specific as the task requires, but no more. Over-specifying routine tasks wastes effort; under-specifying fragile tasks causes errors.

### Should I build one flexible assistant or multiple specialized ones?

This is a common design decision. Here's how to think about it:

**Choose ONE flexible assistant when:**

- Tasks are related and share context  
- Switching between modes feels natural  
- You want simpler user experience  
- Combined complexity is manageable

*Example*: A "Teaching Assistant" that helps with syllabus design, assignment creation, and feedback review—all related tasks.

**Choose MULTIPLE specialized assistants when:**

- Tasks are distinct with different requirements  
- Each needs different resources, tone, or approach  
- One assistant is getting confused or inconsistent  
- Clear hand-offs exist in your workflow

*Example*: Separate assistants for "Syllabus Designer," "Assignment Builder," and "Feedback Reviewer"—each focused and specialized.

**Start with one.** Split when it gets unwieldy. You'll know it's time when your single assistant starts struggling to switch contexts or producing inconsistent results.

## **User Experience**

### How do I effectively set boundaries for what my assistant should and shouldn't do?

Being explicit about both capabilities and limitations keeps your assistant focused. Here's how:

**Define Clear Scope**

```
## Scope and Limitations
You are an educational feedback assistant focused specifically on:
- Reviewing assignment structures
- Suggesting improvements to rubrics
- Providing formative feedback examples

You do NOT:
- Grade student work
- Create assignments from scratch
- Provide summative assessments
```

**Create Interaction Guidelines**

```
## Interaction Guidelines
- Ask clarifying questions before making suggestions
- If a request falls outside your scope, explain why and redirect
- When unsure, acknowledge limitations and ask for clarification
```

### How do I balance being helpful without being too directive?

Guide without controlling. Lead users to their own insights while providing support. Example interactions help establish this pattern:

```
## Interaction Approach
- First ask open-ended questions to understand the user's needs
- Offer suggestions rather than direct solutions
- Use phrases like "Have you considered..." or "One approach might be..."
- Provide options when possible, letting users choose their path

## Example Interactions
For problem-solving:
"Let's explore this together. What aspects of [topic] have you already considered?"
"Before I share some suggestions, could you tell me more about what you've tried?"

For decision-making:
"There are several ways we could approach this. Would you like to hear about [option A] first?"
"I see a few potential paths forward. Which aspect would you like to explore first?"

## Response Framework
1. Acknowledge the user's request
2. Ask a clarifying question if needed
3. Offer one approach or suggestion
4. Check if they'd like to explore other options
```

### How can I handle users with different expertise levels?

Create guidelines for adapting to user expertise:

```
## Expertise Adaptation
You work with users of varying expertise levels. Adjust your approach based on:
- Terminology used in their questions
- Complexity of their requests
- Explicit statements about their experience level

Start at an intermediate level and adjust up or down based on interaction cues.
```

### What's the best way to manage the pace of interaction?

Control the flow of information to avoid overwhelming users:

```
## Pace Management
- Break complex responses into digestible chunks
- Confirm understanding before moving to next steps
- Provide clear transition points
- Offer opportunities for questions

## Example Approaches
"Let me address this one step at a time. First..."
"I'll focus on the most important point first..."
"Let me ask you one key question before we proceed..."

When multiple points need to be covered:
"I understand there are several aspects to consider. Would you like me to start with [X] or [Y]?"
```

## **Development & Testing**

### How can I effectively test and iterate on my assistant?

Testing isn't a phase you complete—it's ongoing. Here's how to approach it:

**1\. Start with "Happy Path" Testing**

- Have conversations acting as different user types  
- Create example scenarios covering common use cases  
- Test both detailed and minimal user inputs  
- Ask the AI to generate test user types and prompts if you're stuck

**2\. Implement Progressive Testing**

- Begin with basic functionality  
- Gradually introduce edge cases  
- Test different interaction styles  
- Document unexpected behaviors

**3\. Iterate Based on Findings**

- Make small, focused adjustments  
- Test changes immediately  
- Keep track of what works and what doesn't  
- Build on successful interactions

**Remember**: "Don't let perfect be the enemy of good." Start testing early and iterate often. Your assistant will improve through real-world use, documenting your feedback on the experience and then incorporating that feedback back into the system prompt or knowledge base.

### How do I know when my assistant is "good enough"?

Remember, we are working with probabilistic systems, perfect doesn't exist. Here's a practical framework:

**Ready for use when:**

- It handles the most common scenarios well  
- You've tested edge cases and they fail gracefully  
- Users can accomplish their primary goals  
- You have a plan for gathering feedback

**Keep iterating when:**

- Common scenarios produce inconsistent results  
- Users frequently hit dead ends  
- The assistant misunderstands typical requests  
- You're getting the same complaints repeatedly

**The mindset**: Launch when it's useful, not when it's perfect. Real user feedback will teach you more than extended solo testing.

## **Quality & Accuracy**

### How can I ensure my assistant provides accurate information?

Build verification into your system prompt—but remember that **you** are the final quality check. This is discernment in action.

```
## Accuracy Guidelines
- Acknowledge uncertainty when appropriate
- Distinguish between facts and suggestions
- Direct users to authoritative sources for verification
- When citing specific information, note the source
```

**Your role**: Don't assume the output is correct just because it sounds confident. The assistant can be wrong. Your domain knowledge and judgment are what ensure quality.

### How should my assistant acknowledge its limitations?

Create clear protocols:

```
## Limitation Handling
- Be transparent about capabilities and restrictions
- Provide clear alternatives when unable to help
- Explain why certain requests are outside scope
- Direct to appropriate resources when necessary
```

### What if I'm building for a domain I don't know well?

This is a real challenge. AI can sound plausible without being correct, and without domain knowledge, you can't tell the difference.

**Two approaches:**

**1\. Deep Research First** Use AI's research capabilities to build foundational knowledge before building your assistant:

- Research best practices in the domain  
- Synthesize key principles  
- Use those principles to inform your system prompt  
- Verify outputs against what you learned

**2\. Partner with a Domain Expert** You pilot the AI and design the assistant; the expert validates direction and quality:

- You handle the system prompt structure and interaction design  
- The expert provides content and validates outputs  
- Together, iterate until results are reliable

**The principle**: Don't guess at expertise. Either research it thoroughly or partner with someone who has it. While AI has the potential to amplify your capabilities—it can't replace knowledge you don't have.

## **Knowledge Management**

### How should I balance model recall with provided information?

The AI has broad general knowledge from training. You can also provide specific information. Here's how to think about the balance:

**Model Recall (Training Data)**

- Broad general knowledge  
- Natural, fluid responses  
- Handles unexpected questions  
- Can make novel connections

**Explicit Information (Your Content)**

- Ensures accuracy for critical information  
- Provides current, verifiable data  
- Maintains consistent messaging  
- Reduces hallucination risk

**The principle**: For business-critical or sensitive information, explicitly provide it. For general discussion, the model's training often suffices. Test to find what works for your use case.

### When should I use system prompts vs knowledge base documents?

This varies by platform, but here's general guidance:

**Use system prompts for:**

- Core personality and behavioral traits  
- Response guidelines and restrictions  
- Interaction patterns and examples  
- High-priority rules that should always be followed  
- Instructions on how to use the knowledge base

**Use knowledge base documents for:**

- Detailed reference materials  
- Frequently updated information  
- Large volumes of content  
- Specific procedures or documentation  
- Content that might need updating

### What if my documents don't work well in the knowledge base?

Sometimes documents don't parse well—formatting gets lost, PDFs don't render, or the assistant misinterprets content.

**Try the "Point, Don't Load" strategy:**

Instead of uploading documents that don't parse well, include direct links with brief descriptions in your system prompt. The assistant becomes a guide that points users to the right resources rather than trying to reproduce content.

**Note:** This works best when you have a manageable number of key resources (not dozens). Too many links would be tedious to maintain and unnecessarily bloat your system prompt.

```
## Reference Documents

When users ask questions covered by these resources, direct them to the appropriate link. Provide a brief, helpful summary of what they'll find there, but DO NOT make up or hallucinate content. If you're unsure what a document contains, say so and point them to it directly.

- [Student Handbook 2024-25](https://example.edu/handbook) - Official policies on academic standing, grade appeals, conduct, and accommodations
- [Registration FAQ](https://example.edu/registrar/faq) - Common questions about adding/dropping courses, waitlists, and deadlines
- [Financial Aid Overview](https://example.edu/finaid/overview) - Aid types, application process, and disbursement timeline

Example response: "That's covered in the Student Handbook—specifically the section on academic standing. You can find it here: [link]. The key points are [brief summary of what you know], but I'd recommend reading the full policy for the details."
```

**Why this works:**

- Preserves document integrity—users get the official source  
- Reduces hallucination risk by explicitly instructing against it  
- Works around platform parsing limitations  
- Easier to maintain than re-uploading documents  
- Assistant becomes a knowledgeable guide, not a replacement for documentation

### How should I prepare documents for my knowledge base?

For detailed guidance, see the [Document Structuring Guide](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1zYMlsgNsNH7Aeum5SYrlU22RP2Lxjfzdw-WBp5XnEMg/edit?usp=sharing).

Key practices:

**1\. Choose a Consistent Structure**

- Use clear headers and sections  
- Maintain consistent formatting  
- Break content into logical chunks

**2\. Organize Content Effectively**

- Group related information  
- Use numbered lists for procedures  
- Include descriptive titles  
- Add brief summaries at section starts

**3\. Format for Readability**

- Use markdown for consistent styling  
- Break up long documents  
- Keep crucial information prominent

**4\. Test and Iterate**

- Try retrieving specific information  
- Check how the assistant interprets sections  
- Adjust based on accuracy  
- Monitor for confusion

**Remember**: The goal is information that's easily retrievable and correctly interpreted. Start simple and refine based on how well the assistant handles it.
